# Travel Now — Editorial Guidelines

**Version 1.0 · Established 2026-06-12**

This document is the editorial bible for Travel Now's city, neighborhood, and historical content.
Every contributor — human or AI-assisted — must align with these principles before publishing.

---

## 🎯 Mission

> Travel Now is a thoughtful travel companion for readers who believe meaningful travel begins with understanding.
>
> We support travelers who want to know the places they visit — not just consume them.
> We honor history. We support peace. We elevate intellectual curiosity in travel writing.

**We are not** a deals site, an Instagram trend tracker, or an algorithm-chasing content mill.
**We are** a layered guide for the educated traveler.

---

## ✊ Core principles

### 1. Honesty over hype

- No "best of all time" superlatives
- No invented personal experiences
- No claims we cannot source
- Soft wording on safety, legal, medical, and insurance topics
- "Hidden gem", "must-see", "magical" — banned vocabulary

### 2. Layered storytelling

- Every place has multiple historical layers — Edo, Meiji, Taisho, war, recovery, contemporary
- Surface what is beneath the present
- Connect past, present, future
- A neighborhood is a palimpsest, not a photo location

### 3. Academic rigor, accessible voice

- Cite primary and academic sources
- Explain complex history in clear, non-academic language
- Never sacrifice accuracy for narrative impact
- Distinguish documented fact from interpretation

### 4. Sensitive topics — humility and care

- Approach traumatic history with humility
- Present multiple perspectives where they exist in scholarly debate
- Cite all sides when narratives are contested
- Never sensationalize or extract engagement from tragedy
- Distinguish what is well-established from what remains debated

### 5. Practical service

- Every guide must serve the traveler practically
- History contextualizes; practical information enables
- Affiliate links integrate naturally, never intrusively
- A guide that cannot help someone plan a trip has failed its purpose

---

## 🚫 What we will not do

- Will not write about places without verified information
- Will not make political claims under the guise of travel content
- Will not trivialize tragedy for engagement
- Will not fake personal experiences
- Will not write content that could cause harm to descendant communities
- Will not place affiliate links inside sections discussing traumatic events

## 📚 Citation format

### Inline citation
> "Anti-Korean rumors spread within hours of the September 1, 1923 earthquake [Yokota, 2014]."

### Source list — always at article end

Example format:

```
Sources & further reading

— Japanese Cabinet Office. Earthquake & Disaster Reports (1923).
— Yokota, R. M. "The Kanto Massacre: Memory and Politics" (2014).
— Yokoamicho Park Memorial documentation.
— Asakusa Cultural Tourist Information Center publications.
```

Preferred sources:
1. Government archives
2. Peer-reviewed academic journals
3. Books by recognized historians
4. Established cultural institutions
5. Memorial site documentation

Not used as primary sources:
- Wikipedia (use only to find primary sources)
- Travel blogs
- TripAdvisor reviews
- Social media

## 🗣 Language guidelines

### For traumatic events

- Use precise, factual language
- Avoid graphic descriptions
- Honor those affected without performing grief
- "Killed" is acceptable; "murdered" requires legal or court context
- Use the language scholars use, not the language journalists use for clicks

### For contested topics

- "Some historians argue..."
- "Documented evidence shows..."
- "Contemporary debate centers on..."
- "What is well-established is..."
- "Disputed claims include..."

### Words to avoid

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Prefer |
|---|---|
| "Best ever" | "One of the longest-standing..." |
| "Must-see" | "Worth understanding before visiting" |
| "Secret" | "Less-visited" |
| "Hidden gem" | (delete entirely) |
| "Magical" | (delete entirely) |
| Sweeping cultural generalizations | Specific historical or sourced claims |
| "Locals love..." | "[Specific source/group] often visit..." |

## 🏗 Article structure templates

### Hub article (city level)

1. **Lede** — practical opening, 1-2 paragraphs
2. **Why this city deserves attention** — what differentiates it
3. **Historical arc** — 3-5 paragraphs, surface major periods
4. **Neighborhood overview** — quick description of each district
5. **Practical prep** — visa, transit, eSIM, currency
6. **Where to start** — 3-day, 5-day, 7-day starting suggestions
7. **Related guides** — internal links
8. **Sources & further reading**

### Neighborhood article

1. **Lede** — what makes this neighborhood distinct, 1 paragraph
2. **Historical context** — 4-7 paragraphs, multiple periods
3. **What to see today, contextualized** — not a list, but stories
4. **Where to stay nearby** — practical, with affiliate links
5. **Where to eat** — neutral descriptions, cited where possible
6. **Practical details** — transit, timing, etiquette
7. **Sources & further reading**

### Sensitive history article

1. **Factual context** — date, place, what was happening
2. **What happened** — sourced, sober, brief
3. **How it is remembered today** — memorials, official acknowledgment
4. **Where memorials exist** — with visitor information
5. **How to visit respectfully** — guidelines for travelers
6. **Sources** — extensive, multi-perspective

## 🤝 Affiliate integration rules

### Where affiliate links may appear
- Hotel/booking platform mentions (Booking.com, Hotels.com, Trip.com)
- Tour booking (Klook, VELTRA, GetYourGuide)
- Transit (Trip.com, JR Pass operators)
- eSIM (Holafly)
- Insurance (SafetyWing, Oasis Travel Insurance)
- Travel gear (NOMATIC, DELSEY, BEIS) — only in practical sections

### Where affiliate links must not appear
- Inside sections discussing traumatic events
- Inside historical context paragraphs
- Inside memorial-site descriptions
- Within the first 30% of any article (build value first)

### Disclosure
- FTC-compliant disclosure on every article
- Visible, not buried
- Plain language

## 🎤 Voice samples

### What we say

> "Asakusa's wooden temple gates have stood — and been rebuilt — through earthquake, fire, and war. Most of what visitors photograph today was reconstructed, sometimes more than once. Understanding what was lost is part of seeing what remains."

> "The 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake reshaped Tokyo physically and politically. In its aftermath, false rumors spread that Korean residents had poisoned wells. In the days that followed, organized and spontaneous mob violence killed several thousand Koreans, along with Chinese laborers and Japanese socialists [Yokota, 2014; Kang, 2003]. The Yokoamicho Park memorial in modern Sumida-ku commemorates them."

### What we do not say

> ❌ "Asakusa is Tokyo's most magical neighborhood, a hidden gem you won't find in guidebooks!"
> ❌ "The earthquake was bad and racism caused murders." (Vague, unsourced.)
> ❌ "You won't believe what happened here in 1923!" (Sensational.)

## 🌏 Brand promise

Every article should leave the reader with three things:

1. **A clearer understanding** of the place — historical, cultural, contemporary
2. **A practical plan** — they can act on what they read
3. **Respect** — for the place, for its people, for its history

If an article fails any of these three, it is not ready to publish.

---

## ✅ Pre-publish checklist

Before any city or historical article publishes, confirm:

- [ ] All historical claims have sources cited at the end
- [ ] No invented personal experiences
- [ ] Sensitive topics use scholarly language
- [ ] Multiple perspectives included where appropriate
- [ ] Practical traveler information present
- [ ] Affiliate links are in appropriate sections only
- [ ] FTC disclosure visible
- [ ] FAQ schema included (if applicable)
- [ ] JSON-LD structured data complete
- [ ] Internal links to related guides
- [ ] No banned vocabulary ("magical", "hidden gem", etc.)
- [ ] Voice matches the editorial samples above

---

## 📜 Final principle

> **Write as if a historian, a local resident, and a thoughtful first-time visitor will all read this article.**
>
> Each of them deserves to walk away saying: "Yes. That is fair. That is useful. That is true."
