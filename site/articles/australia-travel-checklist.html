Australia Travel Checklist: What to Prepare Before You Fly

Practical preparation for first-time travelers to Australia — ETA, jet lag, eSIM, money, weather, customs, and a carry-on checklist that actually works on the world's longest passenger routes.

Published 2026-06-13 · 12 min read

Australia is one of the most rewarding — and logistically demanding — destinations for international travelers. Flights are long, the time zones are deep, the electrical plug is unusual, the biosecurity rules at the border are strict, and most visitors need a travel authorisation before boarding. None of this is hard. It just requires preparation. This Australia travel checklist walks through what to sort before you fly.

1. Why Australia requires extra preparation

Australia is geographically far from almost everywhere. A flight from Tokyo runs roughly 9–10 hours; from London, 21 hours or more with one stop; from Los Angeles, 14–15 hours direct. The country also enforces some of the strictest biosecurity rules in the world to protect its unique ecosystem, and most visitors need a visa or Electronic Travel Authority (ETA) before they can board their flight.

A small amount of preparation removes most of the friction. Travelers who arrive unprepared can spend significant time at the airport sorting things that could have been handled in 15 minutes at home.

2. Passport validity

Most countries require a passport to be valid for the duration of stay, though many carriers and destinations have stricter rules. Check your passport's expiry date as soon as you start planning. If it expires within 6 months of your planned return date, consider renewing — some airlines will not let you board with a passport close to expiry, even if the destination technically allows it.

  • Note the expiry date and any associated visa pages
  • Take a clear photo of the photo page, store it offline on your phone, and share a copy with a trusted contact at home
  • Confirm you have at least two clear pages for entry and exit stamps

3. ETA / visa preparation

Most short-stay visitors to Australia from eligible countries need an Electronic Travel Authority (ETA) or a visa. The ETA is electronically linked to your passport and is typically applied for online or through the official ETA app.

Eligibility, processing times, fees, and the maximum length of stay vary by passport and visa type. Always confirm the current requirements directly with the official Australian Department of Home Affairs before booking non-refundable flights.

Important: Some third-party websites charge significant fees to process Australian ETAs on your behalf. The official application is comparatively inexpensive. Always start at the Department of Home Affairs website to avoid overpaying.

ETA / visa preparation checklist

  • Confirm your passport is eligible for ETA on the official website
  • Have your passport, payment card, and email ready
  • Apply at least 2–6 weeks before departure when possible
  • Save the confirmation email and a screenshot offline
  • Bring printed proof if you prefer redundancy

We do not provide immigration advice. The above is preparation guidance only. Final requirements are determined by Australian authorities.

4. Flight duration and jet lag preparation

Australia hosts some of the world's longest passenger flights. The most demanding routes (London to Perth or Sydney with one connection, New York to Sydney via Los Angeles, etc.) can total over 24 hours in transit. Even short-haul routes from within Asia involve 6–10 hour flights and significant time-zone shifts.

Jet lag is largely about light exposure, sleep timing, and hydration. A few simple habits in the days before the flight can make a meaningful difference:

  • Begin shifting your sleep window 30–60 minutes per day toward the destination time zone in the week before departure (when practical)
  • Hydrate well before, during, and after the flight
  • Limit alcohol and heavy meals in the air
  • On arrival, get natural daylight exposure during local daytime hours
  • Pack a quality sleep mask and earplugs (cabin lights stay on for hours)

Long-haul carry-on comfort items

  • Compression socks (may help reduce leg swelling on long flights)
  • Refillable water bottle (fill after security)
  • Lip balm and moisturizer for cabin dryness
  • Layered clothing — cabin temperatures vary
  • Compact travel pillow and blanket scarf

5. eSIM and internet setup

Australia has excellent mobile coverage in cities and along major travel corridors, and limited or no coverage in remote and outback regions. An eSIM lets you arrive with mobile data already active — no need to find a SIM kiosk at Sydney Airport at 6am after a 14-hour flight.

eSIM checklist

  • Confirm your phone supports eSIM (most recent iPhones and many recent Android phones do)
  • Install the eSIM at home, on Wi-Fi, well before departure
  • Choose a plan matching your trip length and expected data usage
  • Save your activation QR code and provider's support contact offline
  • Pack a backup option (your home SIM, or a roaming pass) in case anything fails

6. Money and cards

Australia is one of the world's most cashless societies. Tap-to-pay is accepted almost everywhere from coffee shops to taxis, and contactless cards or mobile wallets are the practical default. That said, having a small amount of Australian dollars on arrival is useful for unexpected scenarios.

Money checklist

  • Notify your bank of travel dates (where required) to avoid blocked transactions
  • Carry at least two payment cards from different networks (e.g. Visa + Mastercard) in case one is declined
  • Check foreign transaction fees on each card; consider a no-fee travel card
  • Withdraw a small amount of AUD on arrival for incidentals
  • Store backup cash and a backup card separately from your main wallet

7. Weather and seasonal packing

Australia's seasons are inverted relative to the Northern Hemisphere. December– February is summer in most of the country; June–August is winter. Climate also varies sharply by region: tropical in the north, temperate in the south, arid in the interior.

Sun protection

Australia has among the world's highest UV intensities. Sunscreen, a hat, and sunglasses are practical essentials almost year-round, even on cloudy days. 8. Medication and essentials

Bring any prescription medication you need, in original packaging with the prescription if possible. Carry medication in your carry-on rather than checked baggage. Australia restricts certain medications even where they are over-the-counter elsewhere; review the official Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) guidance if you are uncertain about a specific product.

  • Carry medication in original labelled packaging
  • Bring a copy of the prescription where applicable
  • Pack enough for the trip plus a few days' buffer
  • Note generic names — pharmacy branding differs by country
  • Travel insurance with medical coverage is worth considering

We do not provide medical advice. Consult a healthcare professional for guidance relating to your specific medications and conditions.

9. Power adapters and electronics

Australia uses Type I plugs with 230V at 50Hz. Travelers from Japan (Type A, 100V), the United States (Type A/B, 120V), Europe (Type C/F, 230V), and most of Asia will need a plug adapter. Most modern phone, laptop, and camera chargers handle 100–240V natively, so usually only the plug shape needs adapting. Confirm voltage range on each device's label before traveling.

Electronics checklist

  • Compact power bank — useful for long sightseeing days
  • USB-C / Lightning cables (at least one spare)
  • Phone, laptop, camera chargers
  • Headphones / earbuds with case

10. Airport and customs preparation

Australia operates one of the world's strictest biosecurity regimes. Food items, plant material, seeds, wooden items, certain animal products, and dairy items must be declared on the Incoming Passenger Card. Penalties for failure to declare are significant. When uncertain, declare the item — declaring is free; not declaring can be expensive.

What you typically must declare

  • Food of any kind (including snacks you forgot were in your bag)
  • Fresh fruit, vegetables, herbs, seeds
  • Plant material, wooden souvenirs, certain handicrafts
  • Dairy products, eggs, meat (raw, cooked, or preserved)
  • Honey and bee products
  • Medication carried in larger-than-personal-use quantities
  • Camping or hiking gear that has touched soil overseas

The Australian Border Force and the Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry publish current biosecurity guidance. Review these before departure, and complete the Incoming Passenger Card honestly.

11. Carry-on checklist

A long flight to Australia is far easier with a well-organised carry-on. Keep anything critical — documents, medication, charger — in your carry-on rather than checked baggage.

Essential carry-on items

  • Passport with ETA / visa confirmation
  • Printed and digital copies of bookings
  • Wallet with payment cards and small amount of cash
  • Phone with eSIM installed and offline maps downloaded
  • Power bank and short charging cable
  • Headphones or earbuds
  • Prescription medication in original packaging
  • Empty refillable water bottle (fill after security)
  • Sleep mask, earplugs, neck pillow
  • Light layer for cabin temperature
  • Toothbrush, small toiletries kit (within liquids rules)
  • Snacks (but check biosecurity rules before disembarking)
  • One change of clothes, just in case
  • Packing accessories like small zip pouches and a cable organizer (affiliate placeholder)

12. Final pre-flight checklist

On the morning of departure, run through this short list. Most of it is confirmation that everything earlier on the list is actually in place.

The final pass

  • Passport in carry-on with ETA / visa confirmation
  • Boarding pass downloaded offline
  • eSIM tested and working before leaving home Wi-Fi
  • Power bank charged
  • Phone, laptop, camera batteries topped up
  • Medications in carry-on, in original packaging
  • Travel insurance details saved offline
  • Home arrangements done (mail, plants, keys)
  • Cash on hand for taxi, parking, or arrival incidentals
  • Incoming Passenger Card review on flight — declare honestly

FAQ: Australia travel preparation

Do I need an ETA or visa to travel to Australia?

Most short-stay visitors from eligible countries need an Electronic Travel Authority (ETA) or visa before flying. Eligibility, fees, and processing times vary by passport. Always confirm current requirements on the official Department of Home Affairs website before booking flights.

How long does an Australia ETA take to process?

Many ETA applications are processed within minutes, but processing times can vary. Apply at least a few weeks before departure where possible. Verify current processing estimates on the official Department of Home Affairs website.

What power plug does Australia use?

Australia uses Type I plugs with 230V at 50Hz. Most travelers from North America, Europe, or Asia will need a Type I adapter. A universal travel adapter covers Australia along with most other destinations.

What is the best eSIM option for Australia?

Multiple eSIM providers cover Australia. Choose a plan that matches your trip length, comparing data per day, total cost, and coverage in the regions you plan to visit. Confirm your phone supports eSIM before purchase.

Is tap water safe to drink in Australia?

Tap water in Australian cities and major towns is generally treated to drinking water standards. In remote and outback areas, drinking water guidance can vary. A reusable water bottle is useful for staying hydrated.

What should I declare at Australian customs?

Australia has strict biosecurity rules. Food, plant material, wooden items, and certain animal products must be declared on arrival. When uncertain, declare the item — penalties for failing to declare can be significant. Review the official Australian Border Force guidance before flying.

Bottom line

Australia rewards prepared travelers. The ETA, the long flight, the unusual plug, the biosecurity declaration — none of these are difficult on their own. The difficulty is forgetting one of them. Run through this checklist a few weeks before departure and again the morning of your flight, and you will arrive in Australia ready for the actual trip.
