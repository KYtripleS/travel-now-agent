Taipei: The Places Worth Your Time

By Casey, Gently Yonder editor

A considered guide to Taipei's landmarks and areas worth visiting — Taipei 101 and Elephant Mountain, Longshan Temple, the National Palace Museum, the night markets, Beitou hot springs and Maokong, and a Jiufen day trip.

Updated 2026-07-10 · 5 min read

Taipei packs an unusual amount into a compact, walkable frame — a landmark tower and quiet temples, mountain trails and steaming night markets, imperial treasures and hillside teahouses, all a short metro ride apart. What follows is how I’d actually spend a first few days, with the places that reward the time and a few of the specific stalls and corners worth seeking out. Much is free or cheap to do independently; for the ticketed sights and the day trips it’s often simplest to book skip-the-line tickets on Klook or the Taiwanese platform KKday, both strong here, and self-guided attraction tickets on Tiqets cover several of the big names.

Taipei 101, and the view from above

The tapered, bamboo-segmented Taipei 101 is the city’s signature, and its observatory — up near the 89th floor via one of the fastest lifts in the world — is the classic first-day orientation. Don’t miss the giant golden mass damper, the 660-tonne pendulum that steadies the tower in typhoons, now a slightly beloved mascot with its own cartoon. Go at opening or late afternoon to soften the crowds, and consider timed entry to skip the queue. Down at the base, the food hall and the Din Tai Fung branch make an easy lunch.

Elephant Mountain, for the free version

For the postcard shot — 101 rising out of the city at dusk — climb Elephant Mountain (Xiangshan), a steep flight of steps straight from Xiangshan MRT. Twenty to thirty minutes up, you reach the cluster of giant boulders where every photographer in Taipei seems to gather at golden hour; it’s a scramble to the top of them for the framed view. It costs nothing, so bring water and go near sunset — the city lights coming on beneath the tower is the memory people keep.

Temples and old Taipei

Longshan Temple, in the old Wanhua (Bangka) district, is Taipei’s most atmospheric working temple — incense in clouds, chanting, and a beautiful layered building where locals come to petition gods for everything from health to romance. Wander the surrounding streets: the restored Bopiliao Historic Block and the herb-scented lanes nearby are a window into the oldest part of the city. For a different, grander mood, the vast Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall presides over Liberty Square, with a changing-of-the-guard ceremony on the hour beneath the seated bronze statue.

The National Palace Museum

If you see one museum, make it the National Palace Museum, home to one of the world’s greatest collections of Chinese art — much of the imperial collection, carried to Taiwan in 1949. Everyone queues for two small, famous things: the Jadeite Cabbage, carved from a single flawed piece of jade, and the uncannily realistic Meat-shaped Stone. Give it a half-day, go early to beat the tour groups, and an audio guide genuinely helps make sense of the sprawl. It sits north of the centre; pair it with the nearby Shilin area.

Night markets, the heart of it after dark

No Taipei visit is complete without a night market, and each has its own character. Shilin is the biggest and most touristy — come for the spectacle and the giant hand-sized fried chicken cutlets that made Hot-Star famous. Raohe, by the Songshan temple, is more compact and, for my money, the most enjoyable; queue at the entrance for the Fuzhou black-pepper pork buns baked in a clay oven — they are worth it. Ningxia is smaller and purely about food: taro balls, oyster omelette, and old-Taipei classics. Go hungry, carry your EasyCard and some cash, and graze; a bubble tea — invented in Taiwan — is the proper finish.

Green edges: gondolas and hot springs

Two easy escapes sit at the ends of metro lines. Beitou, in the north, is a hot-spring valley: soak in the public Millennium Hot Spring, admire the steaming, jade-green Thermal Valley, and see the award-winning wooden Beitou Public Library, reached on the charming little Xinbeitou branch train. Maokong, reached by a gondola from the zoo (ask for the glass-floored “Crystal Cabin”), is tea-plantation country, with hillside teahouses looking back over the city — sit with a pot of local tie guan yin as the light fades. Both make a gentle half-day.

A day trip: Jiufen and the lanterns

When you have a spare day, the old gold-mining town of Jiufen clings to a hillside above the northeast coast — narrow lantern-lit lanes, teahouses, and sea views. Climb Shuqi Road, the steep stone staircase lined with red lanterns, and take tea at the famous A-Mei Teahouse perched over it. Be honest about crowds: it’s popular, best in the late afternoon once the day-tour buses thin. It pairs with the Pingxi railway line, where you write a wish on a paper sky lantern and release it — touristy, yes, and still quietly moving. You can do it all by train and bus with patience, or fold the stops together on a small-group Jiufen and Pingxi day tour.

How to hold it all

You cannot exhaust Taipei in a first visit, and you shouldn’t try. Anchor each day with one big thing — a tower, a museum, a temple — leave room to wander a market or an old lane, and keep an evening for the hills or the hot springs. For where to stay and how to move around, see our first-timer’s guide to Taipei and the Getting Around Taiwan guide. As ever, opening hours and ticket prices shift, so confirm the specifics close to your visit.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best free view of Taipei 101?

Walk up Elephant Mountain (Xiangshan), a short but steep flight of steps from the MRT. It costs nothing, takes twenty to thirty minutes of climbing, and gives the postcard view of the tower rising out of the city — go near sunset and bring water.

Which Taipei night market is best?

Shilin is the biggest and best known; Raohe is more compact and, for many visitors, more enjoyable; Ningxia is smaller and food-focused. Go hungry, carry your EasyCard, and graze — this is where the city feels most alive after dark.

Is the National Palace Museum worth visiting?

Yes, if you see one museum in Taipei. It holds one of the world's great collections of Chinese art and imperial treasures. Give it a half-day and consider a guided visit or audio guide to make sense of the highlights.

Is Jiufen worth a day trip from Taipei?

Jiufen, an old gold-mining town on the northeast coast, is the classic day trip — lantern-lit lanes, teahouses, and sea views, best in the late afternoon. It is reachable by train and bus, or via an easy small-group tour that folds in nearby stops like the Pingxi line.
